Top Destinations You Can’t Miss


  • The Dagdusheth Halwai Ganpati Temple in Pune stands as one of Maharashtra’s most revered and iconic religious landmarks. Founded in 1893 by Dagdusheth Halwai, a successful sweet maker and businessman, the temple was built as a tribute to Lord Ganesha after Dagdusheth and his wife Lakshmibai lost their only son to a devastating plague epidemic. Guided by their spiritual guru, Madhavnath Maharaj, the grieving couple constructed the temple as a means of solace and healing, and the first Ganesh idol was crafted by artist Balaji Patole.

  • The temple is renowned for its five-and-a-half-foot tall idol of Lord Ganesha, lavishly adorned with gold and jewels, which is a major draw for devotees and tourists alike. The idol, celebrated for its beauty and spiritual aura, is insured for a significant sum, reflecting its immense value to the community. The temple’s location on Laxmi Road, near the historic Shaniwar Wada fort, makes it easily accessible and a central part of Pune’s cultural landscape.

  • Dagdusheth Ganpati Temple is especially famous for its Ganesh Chaturthi celebrations. During this ten-day festival, thousands of devotees from across India and the world visit to participate in elaborate rituals, processions, and cultural events. The temple played a pivotal role in the transformation of Ganesh Chaturthi into a public festival, thanks to the efforts of Indian nationalist Lokmanya Tilak, who saw it as a means to unite people during the freedom struggle

  • Shaniwar Wada is a historic fortification located in Pune, Maharashtra, built in 1732 by Peshwa Bajirao I, the prime minister of the Maratha Empire. It served as the official seat and residence of the Peshwas, who were the de facto rulers during the Maratha rule. The name "Shaniwar Wada" translates to "Saturday Fort," as its foundation stone was laid on a Saturday.

  • Originally intended to be a grand seven-storied palace constructed entirely of stone, the fort's upper floors were ultimately built with bricks and wood due to restrictions imposed by Chhatrapati Shahu, the Maratha king, who decreed that only the king could authorize stone constructions. This decision later contributed to the fort's vulnerability during the British artillery attack in 1818, which caused the collapse of the upper stories, leaving only the stone base intact.


  • Sinhagad Fort located about 25 kilometers southwest of Pune, Maharashtra, is a historic hill fort perched at an elevation of approximately 1,312 meters above sea level on the Sahyadri mountain range. Originally known as Kondhana, the fort has a rich history dating back over a thousand years, with its earliest construction attributed to the Rashtrakutas in the 8th century CE. Over centuries, it changed hands among various rulers, including the Yadavas, Bahamanis, Mughals, and Marathas.

  • The fort is most famous for the Battle of Sinhagad in 1670, when Shivaji Maharaj’s trusted general, Tanaji Malusare, led a daring nighttime assault to recapture the fort from the Mughals. Tanaji scaled the steep cliffs with the help of a monitor lizard named Yashwanti and fought valiantly but lost his life in the battle. Shivaji’s lament, “Gad ala pan sinh gela” (“The fort is captured, but the lion is lost”), immortalized Tanaji’s sacrifice and led to the fort being renamed Sinhagad, meaning “Lion’s Fort”.

  • The BAPS Shri Swaminarayan Mandir in Pune is a magnificent Hindu temple located in the Narhe suburb, about 14 kilometers from Pune Junction along National Highway 48. Dedicated to Bhagwan Swaminarayan, it is part of the Bochasanwasi Akshar Purushottam Swaminarayan Sansthan (BAPS) network, which oversees more than 1,100 temples worldwide. The temple was inaugurated in February 2017 after an impressive construction period of just 24 months, showcasing remarkable architectural and cultural heritage.

  • Architecturally, the Mandir is a stunning example of traditional Nagara style combined with Southern Shilpa Shaili craftsmanship. The entire structure is carved from pink sandstone and red stone sourced from Rajasthan, with no steel used in its construction. The temple covers approximately 33,523 square feet, measuring 184.6 feet in length, 181.6 feet in width, and rising 74.6 feet high. It features 23 samrans (domes), including seven large ones, 140 intricately carved pillars, and 109 beautifully sculpted torans (decorative arches). The temple’s detailed carvings include over 10,000 sculptures and deities, reflecting the rich cultural heritage of Maharashtra and India.


  • Prem Mandir in Pune is a beautiful and spiritually significant temple dedicated to Lord Krishna and Radha, often referred to as a "Temple of Divine Love." Unlike the famous Prem Mandir in Vrindavan, the Pune temple is relatively new and located in the Katraj area, where it is also known as the ISKCON Prem Mandir. It was established under the inspiration of Jagadguru Kripaluji Maharaj, a revered spiritual leader who emphasized devotion and love as central themes of spirituality.

  • The temple’s architecture is a blend of traditional Indian styles with intricate carvings and sculptures that depict various divine pastimes of Radha and Krishna. The serene environment is enhanced by well-maintained gardens, fountains, and water bodies surrounding the temple, creating a peaceful ambiance for devotees and visitors. The temple is a popular spot for spiritual seekers, families, and tourists who come to experience its tranquil atmosphere and participate in devotional activities such as aarti, bhajans, and meditation.
